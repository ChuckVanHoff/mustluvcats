WHAT'S FOR LUNCH?

ABOUT OUR PROJECT

“Ask not what you can do for your country. Ask what’s for lunch.” ― Orson Welles


For centuries humans have been exploring the relationship between our health and the food that we eat. There have been weight loss diets recommended by (often questionably qualified) individuals going as far back as William Banting's 1864 book, "Letter On Corpulence." Amazon currently boasts more than 50,000 books on diet and weight loss.

At the time of this project's deployment, medical journal The Lancet published the results of a 27-year global study of the relationship between diet and chronic, non-communicable diseases. The conclusion? As the New York Times succinctly put it: "Poor Diets Linked to One in Five Deaths."

There are as many reasons for dietary monitoring as there are people. Weight loss, weight gain, allergies, athletic training, diseases or disease avoidance. Each diet's restrictions can vary wildly, from no-animal-product vegan to high-fat, extreme low-carb ketogenic, and every variation in between. They are so many with such wildly contradictory advice that it can be impossible to know where to begin.

(For the record, the study above described a poor diet as ""short on fresh vegetables, seeds and nuts but heavy in sugar, salt and trans fats".)

Regardless of the path one chooses, the key to staying on track is knowing what's in the food that you eat. Therefore, we have pulled nutritional data from the USDA's Food Composition Databases and broken out the data into a variety of visualizations to offer more insight into the foods we eat.
